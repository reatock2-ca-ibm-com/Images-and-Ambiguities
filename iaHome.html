My Story

Images and Ambiguity: Life on the Spectrum began as my personal journey of self-discovery. I started this project when I realized that the way I see and interpret the world is different from many others, and that this difference isn't a limitation—it's a unique lens that offers its own insights and understanding.

Why I Started This Journey

For years, I struggled to understand why certain images spoke to me so powerfully while others left me confused. I noticed that what seemed clear to others was often ambiguous to me, and what I found obvious, others missed entirely. Instead of seeing this as a problem, I began to see it as an opportunity to explore a different way of experiencing the world.

Images as My Language

I've always been drawn to visual information. Sometimes, a single image can communicate something to me that a thousand words cannot. Through this project, I'm learning to trust my visual instincts and understand how my mind processes the world around me differently.

Living and Learning on the Spectrum

Being on the spectrum means that my brain works differently, and that includes how I perceive and interpret visual information. Rather than trying to fit into neurotypical ways of understanding, I'm embracing my unique perspective and sharing what I discover along the way.

Understanding Myself

How My Mind Works with Images

I've learned that my brain processes visual information in its own unique way. Sometimes I notice details that others miss completely, and other times I see patterns and connections that aren't immediately obvious. This isn't better or worse than other ways of seeing—it's simply different, and that difference has taught me so much about myself.

When Confusion Becomes Clarity

There are times when an image that confuses everyone else makes perfect sense to me. And there are times when something that seems obvious to others leaves me completely puzzled. I've learned to embrace these moments of ambiguity as opportunities for deeper self-understanding rather than sources of frustration.

Learning to Trust My Perspective

Growing up on the spectrum, I often doubted my own perceptions and interpretations. Through this journey with images and words, I'm learning to trust my unique way of seeing and understanding. My perspective might be different, but it's valid and valuable.

The Gift of Different Seeing

I've come to realize that my spectrum experience gives me access to ways of perceiving and interpreting that many others don't have. This isn't about being better or worse—it's about recognizing that different minds bring different gifts to understanding our complex world.

Living on the Spectrum

Being on the autism spectrum is just one aspect of who I am, but it's an important part of how I experience and interpret the world around me. Through this personal journey, I've learned to appreciate the unique ways my mind works with visual information and language.

My Different Normal

What feels natural and intuitive to me might seem unusual to others, and vice versa. I've learned that there's no single "right" way to process visual information or find meaning in images. My spectrum experience gives me a different baseline for what feels normal and comfortable.

Sensory Experiences

Colors, textures, patterns, and visual details can affect me differently than they might affect others. Sometimes this means I'm overwhelmed by busy images, and other times it means I find incredible beauty and meaning in subtle details that others might overlook.

Communication Through Images

I often find that I can express myself more clearly through visual elements than through words alone. Images help bridge the gap between my internal experience and what I want to share with others. They become a form of communication that feels more natural to me.

Building Understanding

This journey isn't just about understanding myself—it's also about helping others understand what life on the spectrum can look like. Every person on the spectrum is different, but by sharing my experiences with images and meaning-making, I hope to contribute to greater understanding and acceptance.
